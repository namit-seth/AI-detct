Getting a neural network to run on a microcontroller is harder than it sounds.
You are not just squeezing a model into less memory — you are fighting the compiler,
the scheduler, the flash budget, and sometimes the hardware itself, all at once. This
paper looks at three studies published in 2025 that each attack a different piece
of that problem. The first, Ariel-ML [1], builds a Rust-based platform that splits
inference work across multiple CPU cores on low-power MCUs. The second [2]
deploys a voice-command classifier that recognises 23 keywords on an Arduino Nano
with 256 KB of RAM. The third [3] tests several compressed MobileNetV2 models
for detecting people in images on an ARM Cortex-M4, and also measures how much
energy the whole thing uses when sending results over Wi-Fi. None of these papers
talks to the other two. Reading them together, though, a clear picture emerges: 8-bit
quantization is the one technique that always works, multi-core scheduling is being
completely ignored by most frameworks, and the field still has no agreed way to
measure and report energy consumption. This paper maps those findings, identifies
the gaps, and proposes a practical six-step workflow for actually getting TinyML
models deployed on real embedded hardware.
