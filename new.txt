Getting a neural network to run on a microcontroller is harder than it sounds. The challenge extends well beyond memory constraints — it means wrestling simultaneously with the compiler, the scheduler, the flash budget, and the hardware's own peculiarities, all at once. This paper examines three 2025 studies, each targeting a distinct aspect of that problem. Ariel-ML [1] presents a Rust-based framework that distributes inference workloads across multiple CPU cores on low-power MCUs. The second paper [2] achieves voice-command classification for 23 keywords on an Arduino Nano within a 256 KB RAM envelope. The third [3] evaluates a family of compressed MobileNetV2 models for person-detection on an ARM Cortex-M4, while also characterising end-to-end energy costs when transmitting results over Wi-Fi. None of these papers cites the others. Taken together, however, a coherent picture emerges: 8-bit quantization remains the single most reliable compression strategy, multi-core scheduling is almost universally neglected by existing frameworks, and the field still lacks a shared standard for measuring and reporting energy consumption. This synthesis maps those findings, surfaces the open gaps, and derives a practical six-step deployment workflow for TinyML practitioners working with real embedded hardware.
